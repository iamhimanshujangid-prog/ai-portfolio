Why AI Ethics Matters

AI systems are now making decisions that affect hiring, healthcare, finance, and criminal justice. When these systems fail — due to biased training data, hallucinated facts, or unchecked privacy violations — real people bear the consequences.

As an LLM Evaluator with 1 year of hands-on experience at TELUS, I've seen firsthand how subtle flaws in AI output can compound into significant harm at scale. This case study examines 3 documented failures and what we should do differently.

"An AI system is only as ethical as the data it learns from, the humans who design it, and the guardrails put in place to catch its mistakes."

Case Studies

03 DOCUMENTED FAILURES · ANALYZED
01

Amazon's AI Hiring Tool — Gender Bias in Recruitment

Algorithmic Bias Hiring

The Problem

Amazon developed an AI-powered recruitment tool to automatically screen resumes and identify top candidates. The tool was trained on 10 years of historical hiring data — data that reflected Amazon's own male-dominated workforce. The AI learned to penalize resumes that included the word "women's" (e.g., women's chess club) and downgraded graduates of all-women's colleges.

What AI Did Wrong

  • Replicated historical gender bias from training data as if it were a desired pattern
  • Penalized gender-coded language without any explicit instruction to do so
  • Operated opaquely — recruiters trusted scores without understanding why
  • Was not audited for discriminatory patterns before deployment

How to Fix It

  • Audit training data for historical demographic imbalances before model training
  • Use bias detection tools (IBM AI Fairness 360) post-training
  • Apply blind screening — remove gender, name, institution signals
  • Human review mandatory for shortlisting decisions
  • Regular adversarial testing with synthetic diverse resumes
Real Impact Amazon scrapped the tool entirely in 2018 after internal audits revealed systematic discrimination — thousands of qualified women may have been filtered out.
02

ChatGPT Invents a Legal Case — AI Hallucination in Court

Hallucination Legal / High Stakes

The Problem

In 2023, a New York lawyer used ChatGPT to research legal precedents for a court filing. ChatGPT confidently cited 6 specific court cases — complete with case names, docket numbers, and quoted rulings. When the judge requested the actual documents, none of the cases existed. They were entirely fabricated by the model. The lawyer, who did not verify the AI output, faced sanctions.

What AI Did Wrong

  • Generated plausible-sounding but completely false citations with high confidence
  • Did not flag uncertainty or inability to verify real-time legal databases
  • No disclaimer that outputs required human verification
  • Prioritized fluency over factual accuracy in a high-stakes domain

How to Fix It

  • Domain-specific AI must be grounded via RAG (Retrieval Augmented Generation) against verified databases
  • AI must express calibrated uncertainty — "I cannot verify this" instead of confident fabrication
  • Mandatory human expert review before submission in legal, medical, financial contexts
  • Add system-level warnings: "Always verify AI-generated citations independently"
Real Impact The lawyers were fined $5,000 each. The case became a landmark warning for AI use in professional legal practice globally.
03

Samsung Data Leak — Employees Feed Confidential Code to ChatGPT

Privacy Breach Corporate Security

The Problem

Within weeks of Samsung permitting employees to use ChatGPT for productivity, three separate incidents occurred: an engineer pasted confidential semiconductor source code asking for bug fixes, another shared internal meeting notes for summarization, and a third uploaded restricted test sequences. All this data was sent to OpenAI's servers — potentially used to improve future models — beyond Samsung's control.

What AI Did Wrong

  • No built-in mechanism to detect or warn when sensitive/proprietary data is submitted
  • Data retention policies were unclear to end users at point of use
  • Tool deployed company-wide without training employees on data security risks
  • No data classification layer to prevent IP from leaving the organization

How to Fix It

  • Deploy on-premise or private AI models for sensitive enterprise use (Azure OpenAI with data isolation)
  • Mandatory AI usage policy training before tool access is granted
  • DLP (Data Loss Prevention) filters to block classified data from reaching external APIs
  • Prompt-level warnings when PII or code patterns are detected in input
Real Impact Samsung banned ChatGPT company-wide after the incidents. Multiple Fortune 500 companies introduced similar bans within months, reshaping enterprise AI policy globally.

Key Learnings

WHAT I TOOK AWAY AS AN LLM EVALUATOR

Data Reflects History, Not Truth

Training data carries societal biases. Models don't just learn patterns — they amplify them. Bias auditing must happen before deployment, not after harm is done.

Confidence ≠ Correctness

LLMs can be fluently wrong. High-confidence hallucinations are more dangerous than obvious errors. Calibrated uncertainty and source grounding are non-negotiable in high-stakes domains.

Users Need Guardrails, Not Just Trust

Most AI misuse is not malicious — it's uninformed. Proper onboarding, usage policies, and technical safeguards prevent the majority of AI-related incidents.

Human Oversight Is Non-Negotiable

AI should augment human judgment, never replace it entirely in consequential decisions. The lawyer who submitted AI output without verification made a process error, not just a tech error.

Privacy by Design, Not Afterthought

Data governance must be built into AI tool rollouts. Employees will use the path of least resistance — it's the organization's job to make the safe path also the easy path.

RLHF & Evaluation Are Critical

My experience evaluating LLM outputs at TELUS showed me that systematic human feedback loops — when done rigorously — catch exactly the types of failures in these case studies before they reach users.
